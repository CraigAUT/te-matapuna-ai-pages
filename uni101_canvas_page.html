Pono · Tika · Aroha

Te Aronui — AUT's Te Tiriti framework — gives us three values that guide how we learn, work, and make decisions.

The following definitions are drawn directly from Te Aronui: AUT Te Tiriti Framework, Tier 2.

Pono

The why

Fact · Truth · Reality · Integrity

We are truthful, honest, and sincere in our interactions. Our integrity acknowledges there can be differing interpretations — pono helps us navigate this space.

Desired outcome: To communicate clearly with integrity and purpose.

Tika

The what

Act accordingly · Ethical expression

Tika is a physical manifestation of pono. We act in accordance with what we believe is right. Ethical expression considers the consequences for everyone affected.

Desired outcome: Any action is considered and appropriate.

Aroha

The how

Empathy · Care · Compassion

Aroha determines how pono and tika are expressed and received. If pono is the why, and tika is the what, aroha is the how. It ensures mana and relationships are not diminished.

Desired outcome: Mana is restored, intact or enhanced.

Three questions before you use AI

Pono: Is this genuinely my thinking — my truth?

Tika: Is this the right tool, used in the right way, for the right purpose?

Aroha: Am I genuinely caring for my own learning — and being fair to others?

If all three are yes — proceed. If any is no — stop and reconsider.

The whakapapa of ideas

Every idea has a lineage. Academic work honours that lineage.

Whakapapa is the Māori concept of genealogy — tracing where things come from, how they connect, and who they belong to. In academic writing, every idea, argument, and piece of evidence has a whakapapa too: a history of thinkers, researchers, and writers whose work built on each other's.

When you reference a source, you are acknowledging that lineage. You are saying: this idea came from here, and I am building on it honestly.

How AI can disrupt the whakapapa of ideas

When AI summarises a source for you, or generates text that sounds like knowledge, it cuts the chain. You end up working with ideas that have been filtered, flattened, or fabricated — with no way to trace where they actually came from.

Fabricated references — AI can produce references that look real but don't exist. If you cite them, you are breaking the whakapapa of ideas entirely.
Summarised sources — When AI abstracts a text for you, the original author's argument gets filtered. You may end up misrepresenting what they actually said.
Generated text — AI-produced sentences have no whakapapa. They cannot be attributed to any thinker or verified through any source. Submitting them as your own analysis is a break in integrity at every level.

Using AI while honouring the whakapapa

You can use AI in ways that support your engagement with the whakapapa of ideas — rather than bypassing it.

Use AI to help you find your own words for ideas you have already understood from your reading.
Use AI to check your understanding of a concept — then go back to the original source to verify.
Always find and read your sources yourself. The connection between you, the idea, and the original thinker is yours to make.

The AI decision game

We played this in class — here's how to keep using it.

In your workshop, you worked through a set of real scenarios — situations students actually face when using AI in their research process. For each one, you decided: YES, NO, or IT DEPENDS.

The game doesn't have a simple answer key. Most of the interesting decisions live in it depends — and the values of pono, tika, and aroha are the tools you use to work out what it depends on.

YES

The thinking is yours. AI is the tool, not the author. You can explain and defend every part of your work.

IT DEPENDS

Ask yourself: which value is at stake here? What would pono, tika, or aroha say about this?

NO

AI has done the thinking, the finding, or the writing. Your name is on work that isn't genuinely yours.

The scenarios in the game map directly to the stages of Assessment 2 — you'll find them reflected in the guidance below.

Stage 1 — Developing your research question

From a broad topic to a focused, researchable question.

Your lecturer has given you a broad topic. Your first job is to narrow it down into a research question — specific enough to investigate in 1000 words, broad enough to find sources on.

Step 1 — Narrow the topic with AI as a thinking partner

AI can help you explore different angles on a broad topic — but the choice of direction has to be yours. You need to be the one who decides what interests you, what's relevant to your course, and what's actually researchable.

Try this prompt in Copilot:

I've been given the broad topic: [your topic here]. I'm writing a 1000-word academic analysis. What are three or four different angles I could take on this topic? For each angle, briefly explain what kind of question I'd be investigating.

Pono check: Read the suggestions critically. Which one genuinely interests you? Don't just use the first one — choose the direction that you can write about with curiosity.

What makes a good angle to pursue? ▾

A good angle has three qualities:

It's specific enough that you're not trying to cover everything about a broad topic.

It's researchable — there are academic sources that speak to it directly.

It's genuinely yours — you can bring your own thinking and perspective to it, not just summarise what others say.

Step 2 — Draft and test your research question

Once you have an angle, draft a research question yourself — then use AI to test whether it's the right scope for this assessment.

Try this prompt in Copilot:

Here is my draft research question: [your question]. I'm writing a 1000-word academic analysis. Is this question too broad, too narrow, or about the right scope? Explain why.

Aroha check: Is the feedback useful? Does it make sense to you? You decide whether to adjust the question — not AI.

The four steps of a strong research question ▾
1. Define the topic area — In a sentence or two, describe your broad topic.
2. Describe the problem — What issue or question does your angle address?
3. Specify the gap — What is it that you're actually trying to find out?
4. Write the question — Start with what, why, how, or to what extent. Avoid yes/no questions.

Pono — Does this question genuinely reflect your own curiosity and direction?  ·  Tika — Is it appropriately scoped for what you've been asked to do?  ·  Aroha — Will you be able to write 1000 words about this with genuine engagement?

Stage 2 — Finding and evaluating sources

From your research question to keywords, search strings, and quality sources.

Finding your own sources is not just a rule — it's how you develop a genuine understanding of what's out there on your topic. AI cannot reliably do this for you, and the risks of letting it try are significant.

Step 1 — Identify keywords and related terms

Start by pulling the key concepts out of your research question. Then use AI to help you think of synonyms and related terms — different ways of saying the same idea that might appear in different sources.

Try this prompt in Copilot:

My research question is: [your question]. What are the key concepts in this question? For each concept, give me a list of synonyms or related terms I could use when searching for academic sources.

Tika check: Do these terms make sense for your topic? Add any that feel right, ignore any that don't. You know your topic better than AI does.

Why do synonyms matter in academic searching? ▾

Different authors use different words for the same concept. For example, a researcher writing about young people might use adolescents, youth, or teenagers instead. If you only search one term, you miss everything written using the others.

Building a list of synonyms before you search means you cast a wider, more strategic net.

Step 2 — Build a search string

A search string combines your keywords using Boolean operators — AND, OR, NOT — to make your search more precise and more powerful. Take the keywords from Step 1 and ask AI to help you combine them.

Try this prompt in Copilot:

Here are the keywords for my research question: [your keywords and synonyms]. Can you combine these into a Boolean search string using AND, OR, and brackets? Then explain what the search string is doing in one sentence.

Aroha check: Read the one-sentence explanation. Does it match what you're actually looking for? If you understand what the string is doing, you can adapt it next time without help.

How Boolean operators work ▾
AND — narrows your search. Both terms must appear. climate change AND youth finds sources about both.
OR — widens your search. Either term can appear. (youth OR adolescent OR teenager) finds sources using any of these words.
Brackets — group your OR terms together so the database interprets them correctly.
Example: ("climate change" OR "global warming") AND (youth OR adolescent) AND "mental health"

Step 3 — Run your search in the Library, not in AI

This is the step that has to be yours. Take your search string to Library Search or AUT Google Scholar — not back to Copilot.

Why not just ask AI for sources directly?

AI tools — including Copilot — can produce references that look real but don't exist. This is called hallucination, and it happens even with confident, well-formatted citations. If you submit a reference that doesn't exist, that is fabrication — regardless of whether you knew.

Pono — truth and reality. Always verify sources exist in Library Search before you use them.

Tips for searching in the Library ▾

Use the Peer-Reviewed filter to limit results to academic journal articles.

Use the Date filter to find recent sources — check your assignment requirements for how recent sources need to be.

If your search returns too many results, add another AND term to narrow it. If too few, remove a term or use more OR synonyms.

Not finding what you need? Ask a librarian — that's exactly what they're there for.

Step 4 — Evaluate what you find

Not every source you find will be useful or credible. Before you read anything in depth, ask four questions drawn from Te Whatu Aho Rau — a Māori-informed framework for evaluating information:

Pūtaketanga — Origin

Who created this? When, where, and for whom?

Aronga — Perspective

What is the purpose of this source? Is there a bias or position I should be aware of?

Tātai Hononga — Connections

How does this connect to my topic and other sources I've found? Whose voices are present — and absent?

Māramatanga — Understanding

Does this source genuinely add insight to my research question?

Source: Feekery, A., & Tawhai, R. (2024). Te Whatu Aho Rau – He Anga Arotake: An information evaluation framework.

Pono — Are these sources real, verified, and accurately representing what you say they say?  ·  Tika — Are they appropriate for academic work at this level?  ·  Aroha — Have you actually read them, not just cited them?

Declaring your use of AI

Transparency is not optional — it is part of what pono means in practice.

The Assessment 2 brief states: "Students must cite any AI-generated material that informed their work by following the conventions outlined in Your Library on Canvas: How do I reference and declare all my uses of GenAI?"

This means: any time AI contributed to your thinking, your structure, your wording, or your understanding — you need to declare it. Uncertainty about what counts is not a reason not to declare. It's a reason to ask your lecturer before you submit.

You likely need to declare:

Prompts you used to explore your topic or refine your research question
Keywords or search terms generated with AI assistance
Any AI-generated text that appears in or shaped your writing
Feedback from AI that you acted on in revising your draft

How to declare:

Follow APA 7th referencing conventions for AI tools
Include an in-text reference where AI-generated content appears
Include a full reference in your reference list
See Your Library on Canvas: How do I reference and declare GenAI use?

Pono in action: Declaring too much is never a problem. Submitting work that used AI without any declaration — even if the use was perfectly legitimate — is a pono failure. When in doubt, declare it and explain it.
