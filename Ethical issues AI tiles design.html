Environmental Impact

Energy, water, scale — and our responsibility to Papatūānuku

Kaitiakitanga Tika Whenua Papatūānuku Tangaroa

What's happening

Generative AI runs inside massive data centres using specialised hardware, constant cooling, and round-the-clock power. The environmental story has two phases: training (building the model) and inference (everyday use — where AUT's footprint sits).

AUT AIAC Internal Review (2026)

"AI does have an environmental cost — but understanding that cost requires looking beyond simple comparisons and being clear about what we know, what we don't and what is still evolving."

Training a single large model
≈ 50 GWh — annual electricity of 6,000–7,000 NZ households. Done by global tech firms, not AUT.
AUT's total cloud services (incl. AI)
≈ 71 tonnes CO₂e in 2024 — well under 1% of AUT's total emissions.
Scale is the real driver
Individual queries use small amounts of energy, but billions running continuously is now the primary source of AI energy demand (IEA; MIT; Berkeley Lab).
What we can't yet measure
Cloud providers don't publish per-prompt emissions. "CO₂ per prompt" remains an estimate, not a fact.

Rough energy comparison (estimates only)

💬 Text query — ≈ 0.2–0.3 Wh. LED TV for several seconds.
🖼️ Image — TV for 1–2 min. Up to 1,000× a text query (Luccioni et al., 2023).
🎬 Video — Highest energy use of any generative task.

Rough guides only. Energy varies by model, task, output size, data-centre location, and grid carbon intensity.

Why it matters in Aotearoa

In te ao Māori, our relationship with the natural world is relational, not transactional. Papatūānuku, Tangaroa, and Ranginui are not "resources" — they are tūpuna.

Students recognise this tension: AI reduces physical waste but carries its own hidden footprint. "If you try in some physical way to make it… it's much more tangible… But if instead you do it with [genAI]… you don't see the waste physically" (Vartiainen et al., 2025).

Te Aronui Connection Kaitiakitanga Tika

Kaitiakitanga calls us to "actively protect the natural environment." AI governance is an act of guardianship over whenua, wai, and moana.

Tika requires "ethical expression considers the consequences of all affected" — including consequences we can't immediately see.

The real ethical question is not whether AI uses energy (it does), but how that use is governed and scaled. That responsibility sits with institutions and providers, not individual prompts (AUT AIAC, 2026).

💡 What you can do — practical tips
  • Ask "Do I need AI for this?" — A search, library guide, or tutor might be better.
  • Be specific — fewer retries = less energy.
  • Text over images unless essential — one image ≈ 1,000 text queries.
  • Stop once done — skip the fifth version.
  • Simpler model for simpler tasks.
  • Rebound effect — easier doesn't mean less total energy.

Prompt tip: "Short answer first (max 6 bullets). Only expand if I ask."

Bias & Representation

Whose voices are centred — and who is missing?

Manaakitanga Pono Whanaungatanga Mana Tikanga

What's happening

GenAI learns from predominantly English-language, Western data. The training process divides the world into categories mirroring a small portion of society, reinforcing patterns that shape how the system represents everything from "professor" to "beautiful" (Crawford, 2021; Noble, 2018). Once embedded, these categories become an unquestioned part of AI infrastructure.

Where bias shows up

Images — Stereotyped depictions of cultures, genders, identities.
Language — Te reo Māori and Pacific languages mistranslated or absent.
Knowledge — Western frameworks treated as default. Indigenous perspectives flattened.
Ontology — AI results can impose a worldview that is alien for many (Bowker & Star, 2000).

Classification and model training are "thoroughly political" yet oversight "partly remains outside democratic governance" (Bommasani et al., 2021).

Why it matters in Aotearoa

In a university grounded in Te Tiriti, bias directly affects equity, inclusion, and cultural safety. Users "cannot be oblivious to the model's biases so that they do not unwittingly reproduce them" (Vartiainen & Tedre, 2023).

Te Aronui Connection Manaakitanga Pono Whanaungatanga

Manaakitanga "acknowledges the mana of all our people… through care, generosity, and mutual respect we uplift the status of everyone." When AI misrepresents, it reduces mana.

Pono "acknowledges there can be differing interpretations of the same fact." AI often presents one as truth.

Whanaungatanga "requires tangata whenua and tangata Tiriti to work alongside each other." Centring only dominant perspectives breaks this.

Ask yourself: Whose perspective is this? Whose might be missing? Would someone from a different background read this differently?

💡 Challenge bias — practical tips
  • Cross-check cultural/historical content with trusted sources.
  • Ask for multiple perspectives explicitly.
  • Be careful with any community's content — AI often gets details wrong.
  • Avoid AI images of real cultural practices or taonga.
  • Talk to people with lived experience — not just AI.

Prompt tip: "What perspectives might be underrepresented here?"

Intellectual Property

Creative rights, consent, and mātauranga Māori

Kaitiakitanga Tino Rangatiratanga Aroha Taonga

What's happening

Most GenAI was trained on billions of creative works — writing, art, code, music — scraped without consent. Artists compete against tools trained on their own work. IP rights belong to a handful of corporations (Bommasani et al., 2021; Copyright Alliance, 2025).

Key concerns

Training data — No consent or compensation for creators.
Style mimicry — AI can imitate living artists' distinctive style.
Mātauranga Māori — Indigenous knowledge used without tikanga (WIPO; Te Mana Raraunga).
Ownership — Who owns AI-generated content? Legally unclear.

Students "faced new moral decisions related to copyrights as well as ethical dilemmas" (Vartiainen et al., 2025).

Why it matters in Aotearoa

When AI ingests cultural knowledge outside its proper context, this raises issues of tino rangatiratanga and kaitiakitanga — the right of communities to govern their own taonga. Even when legal, it may not be ethical.

Te Aronui Connection Kaitiakitanga Tino Rangatiratanga Aroha

Kaitiakitanga — "ensure taonga are protected and thrive." Creative works and mātauranga are taonga.

Tino Rangatiratanga — Te Tiriti guarantees self-determination over taonga. Training on Indigenous knowledge without consent undermines this.

Aroha ensures "mana and relationships are not diminished." Respecting IP is aroha in practice.

Ask yourself: Am I comfortable with how this content was created? Would the original creator be? Does this respect the tikanga of the knowledge involved?

💡 Respect creative work — practical tips
  • Don't request "in the style of" living artists.
  • AI = starting point, not final product.
  • Credit AI where required.
  • Check IP policies for AI-generated work.
  • Seek guidance from knowledge holders for cultural content.

Remember: AI outputs may not be copyrightable. Your contribution matters.

Worker Exploitation

The hidden human cost behind AI systems

Tika Aroha Whanaungatanga Mahi

What's happening

AI depends on thousands of workers in the Global South who label data, filter trauma, and train models. Kenyan workers moderating ChatGPT earned less than US$2/hour while exposed to violence and abuse (Perrigo, 2023). Gray & Suri (2019) call this "ghost work" — hidden labour powering AI invisibly.

The hidden workforce

Data labellers — Paid per task, no minimum wage.
Content moderators — Reviewing traumatic content. High psychological harm.
RLHF workers — Low-paid human feedback training AI responses.
Gig conditions — No security, limited benefits, minimal support.

AI is "neither artificial nor intelligent — it is made from natural resources, fuel, human labour, data, and infrastructure" (Crawford, 2021).

Why it matters in Aotearoa

AUT sits in Tāmaki Makaurau — deeply connected to the Pacific. When AI depends on exploitative labour, using it without awareness reproduces those inequities — especially when Pacific workers are disproportionately affected.

Te Aronui Connection Tika Aroha Whanaungatanga

Tika considers "the consequences of all affected" — including invisible workers who built the tool.

Aroha ensures "mana… is not diminished." When workers are invisible, their mana is diminished.

Whanaungatanga requires "mutually beneficial exchanges." $2/hour so we get free text is not mutual.

Ask yourself: What human cost made this tool possible? Would I accept these conditions for someone I care about?

💡 Be an informed user — practical tips
  • Learn about AI companies' labour practices.
  • Advocate for ethical procurement.
  • Support supply chain transparency.
  • Use AI purposefully — casual use has costs.
  • Consider Pacific workers in the chain.

Remember: "AI" is built by people. Their conditions matter.

Surveillance & Privacy

Data collection, consent, and protecting what matters

Kaitiakitanga Tino Rangatiratanga Pono Tapu

What's happening

Your conversations may be stored, analysed, and used to train future models. Most providers retain data by default. Personal, institutional, or culturally sensitive information can be absorbed into systems beyond your or AUT's control (Zuboff, 2019).

What's at risk?

Your data — Prompts stored indefinitely, used for training.
Others' data — Inputting personal info without consent.
Research data — Ethics obligations for confidential data.
Data sovereignty — Māori data governed by Māori (Te Mana Raraunga).

NZ Privacy Act 2020 applies to what staff and students enter into AI systems.

Why it matters in Aotearoa

Personal and collective information can be tapu — sacred and requiring protection. Mana over one's own data is cultural and spiritual, not just legal. When overseas corporations absorb data from Aotearoa, sovereignty questions become urgent.

Te Aronui Connection Kaitiakitanga Tino Rangatiratanga Pono

Kaitiakitanga — "ensure taonga are protected." Data is taonga. We don't hand it to AI without understanding what happens.

Tino Rangatiratanga extends to Māori data sovereignty — the right to govern data about or from Māori.

Pono — "communicate clearly with integrity." If we can't explain where our data goes, we're not acting with pono.

Ask yourself: Would I be comfortable if this prompt became public? Am I sharing someone else's information? Does this data belong to me?

💡 Protect privacy — practical tips
  • Never input names, IDs, health info, or personal details.
  • Use institutional tools with data agreements (e.g., AUTGPT).
  • Opt out of training data collection where possible.
  • Respect Māori data sovereignty.
  • Anonymise all examples and scenarios.

Rule of thumb: If it's sensitive, don't type it into AI.

Over-reliance & Thinking

Keeping your skills sharp and your thinking independent

Pono Tika Te Kete Aronui Ako

What's happening

AI can shortcut thinking that builds understanding. Productive struggle is where deep learning happens. Over-reliance leads to deskilling, shallow knowledge, and automation bias — trusting confident outputs that are wrong (Parasuraman & Manzey, 2010). AI models are "black boxes" — even creators can't fully explain specific outputs.

Risks of over-reliance

Deskilling — Lose abilities you don't practise.
Shallow learning — Getting an answer ≠ understanding.
Automation bias — Confidence ≠ accuracy. Hallucinations are real.
Opacity — Can't see reasoning. Sources often unverifiable.
Dependency — Feeling unable to work without AI.

"Students recognised genAI was influencing their decision making" (Vartiainen et al., 2025).

Why it matters in Aotearoa

Ako — reciprocal learning — positions the learner as active. If AI replaces thinking, it undermines education's purpose. There is "an evident need for pedagogical approaches that help students to engage in critical, creative, and agentive actions" while aware of "underlying power-relationships" (Vartiainen & Tedre, 2023).

Te Aronui Connection Te Kete Aronui Pono Tika

Te Kete Aronui — AUT's name references the basket of knowledge centring "the human experience of discovery." The basket is meant to be carried, not handed to a machine.

Pono asks: are we honest about what we actually understand? "Communicate clearly with integrity" requires genuine knowledge.

Tika — "any action is considered and appropriate." Defaulting to AI isn't considered action.

Ask yourself: Am I learning — or just getting an answer? Can I verify this? Can I explain it in my own words?

💡 Stay capable — practical tips
  • Try first — attempt before turning to AI.
  • AI to check or extend thinking, not replace it.
  • Explain AI output in your own words.
  • Verify all facts, stats, dates, citations.
  • Practise skills without AI regularly.
  • Ask AI to flag uncertainty.
  • Confidence ≠ accuracy.

Prompt tip: "Flag uncertainty. What are you least confident about?"
