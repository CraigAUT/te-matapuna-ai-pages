1

Explore your recipe's research themes

Build a prompt to generate keywords and search strings

▼

Your recipe connects to bigger ideas — food culture, identity, design for storytelling, interactive experiences. Build a prompt that will help you surface those themes and turn them into a search strategy.

🎯 Goal — What do you want the AI to do?

💡 State clearly what you need — keywords, search strings, or both

Identify the key research themes in my recipe project and suggest academic keywords and search strings I can use in library databases.
📚 Context — Your recipe and its significance

💡 Include the recipe, its cultural or personal meaning, and what you're designing

I'm designing an interactive recipe website for butter chicken — a dish my family cooks every Friday night. It came to New Zealand with my grandparents from Punjab. I want to explore the cultural journey of the recipe and how food connects to identity for diaspora communities. This is for a first-year interaction design course.
📋 Expectations — How should the output look?

💡 Specify format, number of themes, and ask for usable search strings

Organise the output as a table with a maximum of 4 research themes. For each theme, provide 3–4 alternative keywords or synonyms. Then combine them into 2 search strings I can paste into AUT Library Search or Google Scholar.
🚧 Limits — What should the AI avoid?

💡 Set boundaries — what to exclude, how to handle uncertainty

Don't write content for my assignment — just help me develop a search strategy. Focus on academic and design research, not recipes. If you're uncertain about cultural context, say so rather than guessing.

Test your search in the AUT Library

See what the AI-generated search strings actually find

▼

AI can suggest search strings, but you need to test whether they work. This is where your critical thinking matters more than the AI's confidence.

  1. Copy a search string from Copilot's response to Activity 1
  2. Go to library.aut.ac.nz — try Library Search, AUT Google Scholar, or a subject database
  3. Paste your search string and review the results
  4. Evaluate: How many results? Relevant? Academic? Recent enough?

💡 Try the same search string in two different places and compare.

3

Refine your search strategy

Build a second prompt to diagnose and fix search problems

▼

You tried something, it didn't work perfectly, and now you're iterating. This is exactly how design works. Use the same four elements to get targeted advice on improving your search.

🎯 Goal — What's the problem?

💡 Too many results? Too few? Wrong topic area?

I'm getting too many results and most aren't relevant to my specific focus. Suggest how I can narrow my search.
📚 Context — What have you tried?

💡 Include your search string, where you searched, and what went wrong

I searched AUT Library for: (food AND culture AND identity) AND (interactive OR digital OR web). I got over 3,000 results. Many are about food science or nutrition, not about the cultural meaning of food or designing interactive experiences.
📋 Expectations — What advice do you want?

💡 Ask for a specific number of suggestions with explanations

Give me 3 specific suggestions to improve my search. For each one, explain why it should help and provide a revised search string I can test.
🚧 Limits — What constraints?

💡 Let the AI know how you want to work — e.g. try one change at a time

Let me try each suggestion before giving more. Focus on search string changes, not general advice. Don't suggest sources directly — I want to find them myself.

➡️ What's next

You've built search prompts, tested them, and refined them — all while thinking about the ethical dimensions. Use these same four elements (Goal, Context, Expectations, Limits) flexibly in your ongoing AI use. You don't always need all four, and they don't need to be in this order.

You lead the conversation, judge what's useful, and you're responsible for everything you submit.
